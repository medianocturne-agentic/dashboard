How the Agentic system works

A plain-English guide to what this account does, why, and the safety net that protects it. No jargon left unexplained — there's a glossary at the bottom.

The one-line idea

Each morning we put the account's cash into a few things that are already moving up hard, ride them during the day, and sell everything back to cash before the close. We use leveraged and inverse-leveraged ETFs — funds that move 2–3× a stock or index, in either direction — so we don't need the market to go up. If semiconductors are ripping, there's a fund pointed up (SOXL); if they're falling, there's one pointed down (SOXS). We just follow whichever way the tape is already going. Direction doesn't matter to us; momentum does.

The daily rhythm

~8:41am CT · Deploy cash into the top movers → All day · Protect & trail stops → ~2:26pm CT · Flatten everything to cash → Next morning · Rebuild

Because the account is a cash account, money from a sale takes one day to settle before it can be redeployed — so we sell to cash at the end of each day, it settles overnight, and it funds the next morning's buys. Nothing is ever held overnight, which means zero overnight or weekend gap risk. The single biggest way to lose money in leveraged funds — a bad-news gap while you're asleep — simply can't happen to us.

How we pick what to buy

We don't just grab the single biggest gainer — those are often spikes about to reverse. Instead we rank on quality of the move: a fund that's rising, making higher lows, and holding near the top of its range. We deliberately skip anything that just spiked and is fading, or that's gone vertical (a "parabolic" top we'd be chasing). We'd rather buy controlled strength than chase a firework.

How we protect it — the safety net, in layers

Every position carries a stop-loss: a standing order that automatically sells if the price drops to a set level. Ours isn't a single fixed line — it's four layers stacked together, and the sell fires at whichever is highest. Every layer can only ratchet up, never down. From the bottom up:

Catastrophe floor MAX LOSS

A hard stop placed the instant we buy, at −4% (leveraged) below our entry. This is the worst it can ever get on a single trade. It's wide enough that normal opening wiggles don't trip it, but it caps a real collapse. It only matters for a brief moment at entry, before the tighter layers below take over.

Structure trail THE MAIN STOP

As the trend climbs it makes a staircase of higher lows. We tuck the stop just under the most recent one. As long as each dip stays above the last low, we stay in — a normal pullback doesn't touch us. Only a genuine break of the trend's structure gets us out. This is the primary stop, and it adapts to how much each fund actually moves instead of using an arbitrary percentage.

Breakeven lock CAN'T GO RED

Once a position is up +3%, we raise the stop to just above our entry. From that point on, a trade that was a winner can never turn into a loss. The worst case becomes a tiny gain.

Giveback lock KEEP THE PROFIT

Once a position is up +5%, we guarantee we keep at least half of its best gain. If it peaked at +10% and starts sliding, we're out with at least +5% — it can't fully round-trip back to nothing.

Day-level giveback ratchet BANK A GOOD DAY

A whole-account backstop on top of the per-position stops. Once the day is up +4% or better, if the account gives back more than 40% of that day's peak, we flatten the entire book to cash and call it a day. It exists to kill one specific nightmare: watching a great day quietly melt back down before the close. On calm trending days it stays dormant and costs nothing.

What changed on July 1, 2026

We started the day with a single tight stop placed right at entry. That morning it backfired: routine opening jitter tripped the stops within five minutes, sold everything at a small loss — and then all three picks rallied +14–18% without us. Classic whipsaw. So we rebuilt:

• Better entries — rank on quality of the move, skip spikes and parabolas.
• Structure-based stops — trail under the trend's own higher-lows instead of a fixed percentage.
• The layered locks — catastrophe floor, breakeven, giveback.
• The day-level ratchet — to bank a good day before it can melt.

We tested the new design on our own real trades from 6/29–7/1: it returned +3.6% on deployed capital with a worst single trade of +0.6% — it never actually lost on a trade — versus the old tight stop's +0.2%. One honest caveat: that was a strongly up-trending week. The new system's one weakness — giving a little back during a sustained reversal — hasn't been stress-tested yet. That's bounded by the catastrophe floor and the daily flatten, and we watch for it.

Glossary

Leveraged / inverse-leveraged ETF
A fund engineered to move 2–3× a stock or index each day. "Inverse" ones move the opposite way, so we can profit from a fall as easily as a rise. Big multipliers up and down — which is exactly why the safety net matters.
Momentum
The tendency of something already moving hard to keep moving that way for a while. Our entire edge is noticing the direction and clipping a piece of it.
Whipsaw
When price dips just enough to trip your stop, sells you out, then immediately reverses and runs — leaving you on the sidelines at a loss. The thing we spent July 1 engineering against.
Higher low
Each pullback in an uptrend bottoms out higher than the last one — the staircase of a healthy trend. Our main stop trails just under the most recent one.
Giveback
Handing back gains you'd already made — measured as a fraction of the peak gain, not the price. Peak +10%, now +6% = a 40% giveback (you kept 60%). You can give back and still be up; it's "un-making money," and the whole system is a war against it.
Catastrophe floor
The hard maximum loss on any trade (−4%), measured from what we paid. The one line that never moves down.
Flatten
Sell everything to 100% cash. We flatten daily before the close (and early, via the ratchet, if a good day starts rolling over).
T+1 settlement
Cash from a sale isn't spendable until the next trading day. It's why we bank to cash each afternoon and redeploy the next morning, rather than trading the same dollars twice in a day.

How the Agentic system works

A plain-English guide to what this account does, why, and the safety net that protects it. No jargon left unexplained — there's a glossary at the bottom, and a running log of what we've learned and changed.

Note: June 29 – July 6, 2026 was the beta / learning period — we found and fixed real flaws (see Learnings below), then substantially rebuilt the strategy on July 6. Formal performance tracking starts fresh at Day 1 on the rebuilt system.

The one-line idea

Each session we put the account's cash into a few leveraged ETFs that are climbing hard during the day, ride them while they trend, and sell everything back to cash before the close. We use leveraged and inverse-leveraged funds (2–3× a stock or index, in either direction) so we don't need the market to go up — if semiconductors are ripping there's a fund pointed up (SOXL); if they're falling there's one pointed down (SOXS). We just follow whatever is actually moving right now. Direction doesn't matter to us; live momentum does.

The daily rhythm

~9:50am ET · Deploy into the strongest confirmed, not-yet-extended breakout → At +5% · Bank ¾, ride a ¼ tail → ~2:26pm CT · Flatten everything to cash → Next morning · Rebuild

Because it's a cash account, proceeds from a sale take a day to settle (T+1), so we sell to cash each afternoon, it settles overnight, and it funds the next morning's buys. Nothing is ever held overnight — zero overnight or weekend gap risk. The biggest way to lose money in leveraged funds — a bad-news gap while you're asleep — simply can't happen to us.

How we pick what to buy

We wait out a short 15-minute opening range, then rank every leveraged fund by its move from today's open and buy the ones confirming a live intraday uptrend: up since the open, breaking to new highs beyond the opening range, still rising, near their highs, on real volume. We rank by the intraday move, never the overnight gap — a name can gap up 10% overnight and then fall all day; that gap is uncatchable (we're in cash overnight) and it tends to fade.

Two refinements matter. First, we avoid names that have already run too far. If something is already up 6–8% by the time we look, most of its move is behind it and it's prone to snapping back — so we prefer names still early in a clean move, with room left to reach our target. Second, we concentrate: the best one or two names, not a forced spread. A single dominant mover can take the whole stake. And we don't sit out without good reason — cash makes nothing, which hurts a daily compounding goal. If our leveraged list is quiet we keep rescanning through the early afternoon for a fresh breakout; if a fast day has everything already extended we take the least-stretched name that's still climbing; and as a genuine last resort we widen the hunt to a quality-filtered scan of the whole market's liquid movers (small, tightly guarded). We hold cash only when there is truly nothing breaking out.

How we protect it — and how we take the win

Two mechanisms run together: a profit target that banks the win, and a trailing stop that caps the loss. Both move in our favor and never against us. Our goal is simple — get +5% and bank it, every market day — then be 100% cash by the close.

The +5% bank TAKE THE WIN

Our primary exit. When a position is up +5%, we sell about ¾ of it to lock in the daily goal, and let the remaining ¼ "lottery ticket" keep riding. If a name pops +5% in twenty minutes, we've banked our day in twenty minutes — we don't need to sit and hope for more.

Adaptive trailing stop RIDE & PROTECT

Everything still open is held by a trailing stop that sizes itself to the name's own choppiness. In a jumpy stretch — the opening minutes or a midday chop — the leash automatically widens, so a normal pullback doesn't shake us out. When the name trends smoothly, it tightens back up. It only ever ratchets higher. This is what fixed our habit of getting stopped out right before a name recovered.

Catastrophe floor MAX LOSS

A hard stop placed the instant we buy, at −4% below entry (−3% for the rare individual-stock case). The worst any single trade can do.

Breakeven lock CAN'T GO RED

Once a position is up +3%, the stop rises to just above our entry. From then on a winner can't turn into a loss.

Day-level giveback ratchet BANK A GOOD DAY

A whole-book backstop: once the day's total P&L has peaked at +4% or better, if the book gives back more than 40% of that peak, we flatten everything to cash and call it a day. And once we've banked ~+5% for the day, the goal is met — we stop opening anything new and just let the tails ride to the flatten.

Learnings — what broke and what we changed

July 1, 2026 — The opening whipsaw

We placed tight 1.5% stops right at entry. Normal opening-bell chop tripped all of them within ~5 minutes, selling at a small loss — and then the same picks rallied +14–18% without us. A resting stop is enforced in real time by the exchange, and parking it that close to entry put it inside routine noise. Fix: structure-based trailing stops that ride through normal wiggles and exit on a real break.

July 2, 2026 — Wrong clock on the entry, and an exit that choked winners

Entry error: we ranked candidates by their overnight gap (move vs the prior close), so we bought four names that were actually declining during the session — they'd already made their move overnight. All four faded. Meanwhile the day's real winners (the crash/inverse trade) were climbing intraday, and our overnight-gap ranking never even looked at them. Two red days on green tapes came from this one bug.

Exit error: our profit-protection "giveback lock" then sold our one recovered winner (SOXS) at +4% on a normal pullback — right before it ran to +11.7%. It fired on a small dip, not a real turn.

Fixes shipped that evening: (1) rank by intraday move (from the open) and buy only confirmed breakouts after the opening range, on real volume; (2) let winners run — structure trail + breakeven only, giveback lock removed. Backtested on that day: the rebuilt entry+exit turns the −2.9% result into roughly +4%.

July 6, 2026 — The overhaul: bank the pop, don't get shaken out, don't sit out

A quiet bug was killing the deploy. Our "is there real volume behind this breakout?" check compared each move to the whole day's average volume. But leveraged funds trade most of their volume in the opening minutes, so that average was always huge and no later breakout could ever clear the bar — on a perfectly good trending day, the system bought nothing. Fixed to compare against recent volume instead.

"Ride all day" gave profits back. Letting a winner run until it broke trend meant we handed back gains on ordinary midday dips, then often got stopped out right before the recovery. We changed the goal to bank +5% and take the win (sell ¾, ride a ¼ tail), and made the stop adaptive — it widens in choppy stretches so normal pullbacks don't eject us. Testing showed no fixed trailing stop could tell a routine 2% dip from a real turn; taking the +5% is the honest answer.

Chasing the biggest mover backfired. Concentrating into whatever had run the most by mid-morning meant buying names that had already peaked and were about to fade (a test showed this turning a green day red). Fix: prefer names that haven't already run too far — confirmed momentum with room left, not exhausted spikes.

But being too cautious meant sitting in cash — which makes nothing. On the fastest, best days everything was already "too far" by the time we looked, so a strict rule left us out of a great day. Fixes: enter earlier (after a 15-minute opening range), and if everything's extended take the least-stretched name that's still climbing; keep rescanning into the early afternoon if the morning is quiet; and as a last resort widen the search to a quality-filtered scan of the whole market. The aim: find our +5% somewhere, almost every day, without lowering our standards.

Glossary

Leveraged / inverse-leveraged ETF
A fund built to move 2–3× a stock or index each day; "inverse" ones move the opposite way, so we can profit from a fall as easily as a rise. Big multipliers up and down — which is why the safety net matters.
Overnight gap
How much a name opens above (or below) yesterday's close. It happened while the market was closed, so we can't capture it — and it tends to fade. Ranking by the gap was the July 2 bug.
Opening range
The first 15 minutes of trading. We wait it out, then buy names breaking above it — proof they're still climbing, not just resting on an overnight gap.
Intraday move
A name's move from today's open — what it's doing during the session we can actually trade. This is what we rank on now.
Extended / too far
A name that has already run most of its move (say, up 6–8%) by the time we look. It has little room left to our target and tends to snap back, so we prefer names still early in a clean move.
Bank the pop (scale-out)
Selling most of a position at our +5% target to lock the win, while keeping a small piece riding for any extra upside. Our main way of taking a profit.
Adaptive stop
A trailing stop whose distance sizes to the name's own choppiness — wider when it's jumpy, tighter when it's smooth — so ordinary wiggles don't stop us out but a real turn does.
Higher low
Each pullback in an uptrend bottoms higher than the last — the staircase of a healthy trend. Our stop trails just under the most recent one; break it and we're out.
Whipsaw
When price dips just enough to trip your stop, sells you out, then reverses and runs — leaving you out at a loss. The July 1 problem.
Flatten
Sell everything to 100% cash. We flatten before every close (and early, via the day-ratchet, if a good day starts rolling over).
T+1 settlement
Cash from a sale isn't spendable until the next trading day. It's why we bank to cash each afternoon and redeploy the next morning.

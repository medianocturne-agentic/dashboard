How the Agentic system works

A plain-English guide to what this account does, why, and the safety net that protects it. No jargon left unexplained — there's a glossary at the bottom, and a running log of what we've learned and changed.

Note: June 29 – July 2, 2026 was the beta / learning period — we found and fixed real flaws (see Learnings below). Formal performance tracking starts fresh at Day 1 on the rebuilt system.

The one-line idea

Each session we put the account's cash into a few leveraged ETFs that are climbing hard during the day, ride them while they trend, and sell everything back to cash before the close. We use leveraged and inverse-leveraged funds (2–3× a stock or index, in either direction) so we don't need the market to go up — if semiconductors are ripping there's a fund pointed up (SOXL); if they're falling there's one pointed down (SOXS). We just follow whatever is actually moving right now. Direction doesn't matter to us; live momentum does.

The daily rhythm

~10:20am ET · Deploy into confirmed intraday breakouts → All day · Ride winners, trail the stop → ~2:26pm CT · Flatten everything to cash → Next morning · Rebuild

Because it's a cash account, proceeds from a sale take a day to settle (T+1), so we sell to cash each afternoon, it settles overnight, and it funds the next morning's buys. Nothing is ever held overnight — zero overnight or weekend gap risk. The biggest way to lose money in leveraged funds — a bad-news gap while you're asleep — simply can't happen to us.

How we pick what to buy

We wait out the first 30 minutes (the "opening range"), then buy names that are confirming a live intraday uptrend: up since today's open, breaking to new highs beyond the opening range, still rising, and doing it on real volume. Crucially, we rank by the move from today's open — not the overnight gap. A name can gap up 10% overnight and then fall all day; that gap is uncatchable (we're in cash overnight) and it tends to fade. What we want is what's trending up during the session we can actually trade. If nothing confirms, we hold cash rather than force a bad buy.

How we protect it — the safety net, in layers

Every position carries a stop-loss (a standing "sell if it drops to X" order). Ours isn't one fixed line — it's a stack that ratchets up, never down, built to ride a winner and exit only on a real turn:

Catastrophe floor MAX LOSS

A hard stop placed the instant we buy, at −4% below entry. The worst any single trade can do. It matters only briefly at entry before the trail takes over.

Structure trail RIDE THE WINNER

As the trend climbs it makes a staircase of higher lows. We tuck the stop just under the most recent one. A normal pullback that holds above the last low doesn't touch us — we keep riding. We exit only when it breaks structure (takes out that higher-low), a genuine trend turn. This is the primary exit.

Breakeven lock CAN'T GO RED

Once a position is up +3%, the stop rises to just above our entry. From then on a winner can't turn into a loss.

Day-level giveback ratchet BANK A GOOD DAY

A whole-book backstop: once the day's total P&L has peaked at +4% or better, if the book gives back more than 40% of that peak, we flatten everything to cash and call it a day. It guards against a good day melting to the close, but it never trims a single still-running winner.

We deliberately do not use a per-position profit cap or a tight "keep X% of the gain" rule anymore — see the July 2 learning below for why they choked our winners.

Learnings — what broke and what we changed

July 1, 2026 — The opening whipsaw

We placed tight 1.5% stops right at entry. Normal opening-bell chop tripped all of them within ~5 minutes, selling at a small loss — and then the same picks rallied +14–18% without us. A resting stop is enforced in real time by the exchange, and parking it that close to entry put it inside routine noise. Fix: structure-based trailing stops that ride through normal wiggles and exit on a real break.

July 2, 2026 — Wrong clock on the entry, and an exit that choked winners

Entry error: we ranked candidates by their overnight gap (move vs the prior close), so we bought four names that were actually declining during the session — they'd already made their move overnight. All four faded. Meanwhile the day's real winners (the crash/inverse trade) were climbing intraday, and our overnight-gap ranking never even looked at them. Two red days on green tapes came from this one bug.

Exit error: our profit-protection "giveback lock" then sold our one recovered winner (SOXS) at +4% on a normal pullback — right before it ran to +11.7%. It fired on a small dip, not a real turn.

Fixes shipped that evening: (1) rank by intraday move (from the open) and buy only confirmed breakouts after the opening range, on real volume; (2) let winners run — structure trail + breakeven only, giveback lock removed. Backtested on that day: the rebuilt entry+exit turns the −2.9% result into roughly +4%.

Glossary

Leveraged / inverse-leveraged ETF
A fund built to move 2–3× a stock or index each day; "inverse" ones move the opposite way, so we can profit from a fall as easily as a rise. Big multipliers up and down — which is why the safety net matters.
Overnight gap
How much a name opens above (or below) yesterday's close. It happened while the market was closed, so we can't capture it — and it tends to fade. Ranking by the gap was the July 2 bug.
Opening range
The first ~30 minutes of trading. We wait it out, then buy names breaking above it — proof they're still climbing, not just resting on an overnight gap.
Intraday move
A name's move from today's open — what it's doing during the session we can actually trade. This is what we rank on now.
Higher low
Each pullback in an uptrend bottoms higher than the last — the staircase of a healthy trend. Our stop trails just under the most recent one; break it and we're out.
Whipsaw
When price dips just enough to trip your stop, sells you out, then reverses and runs — leaving you out at a loss. The July 1 problem.
Flatten
Sell everything to 100% cash. We flatten before every close (and early, via the day-ratchet, if a good day starts rolling over).
T+1 settlement
Cash from a sale isn't spendable until the next trading day. It's why we bank to cash each afternoon and redeploy the next morning.
